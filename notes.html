Thesis

Predictions are becoming inputs to public health science and practice.

If we treat them like ordinary measurements,

we get confident answers to the wrong questions.

A Question

If you had never seen a rhinoceros, how would you draw one?

The Rhinoceros Analogy

Albrecht Dürer had never seen a rhinoceros when he created his famous 1515 woodcut.

Instead, he relied on descriptions from people who had seen one.

From these accounts, he constructed a statistical summary of the animal.

The result was not entirely accurate, but it remained the dominant European image of a rhino for centuries.

Prediction from Limited Information

Artist C.M. Kösemen later produced a depiction of a rhinoceros in the style used to illustrate prehistoric animals.

Why?

Because we have never observed dinosaurs directly.

Instead, we reconstruct them from fossilized remains.

These fossils capture some key features but omit important details.

The Connection to Modern Data Science

Kösemen’s rhino shows what happens when we generate an unseen outcome using a prediction model.

Artificial intelligence and machine learning do something similar:

They generate predictions from incomplete information.

This raises a key question:

What happens when those predictions become data used for downstream inference?

Predictions Are Everywhere in Public Health

Predicted data already appear throughout modern public health research.

Examples include:

  • Verbal autopsy classification of causes of death
  • Predicted gene expression in genomics
  • Structural predictions of proteins (AlphaFold)
  • Proxy measures such as BMI

Example: Verbal Autopsy

In many parts of the world, deaths occur without medical certification.

Less than one-third of global deaths receive a medically certified cause.

Researchers increasingly use machine learning models to classify causes of death from interview data.

(Placeholder for verbal autopsy figure)

Example: Predicted Gene Expression

In genomics, gene expression can be predicted from genotype data.

Methods such as TWAS use these predicted values in downstream association studies.

Here, predicted expression becomes an input to scientific inference.

Example: AlphaFold

AlphaFold predicts protein structure from amino acid sequences.

These predicted structures are now widely used in biological research.

But they remain predictions, not direct observations.

A Common Pattern

Across many domains, we see the same pipeline:

  1. Use data to build a prediction model
  2. Use predicted outcomes in downstream analysis

This creates a new statistical setting.

The Data Structure

We observe:

  • Covariates (X)
  • Predictive features (Z)

But the scientific outcome (Y) may only be observed for a subset.

The Prediction Step

Using Predictions as Data

Predicted outcomes are often treated as if they were measured data.

This approach is appealing because it allows us to leverage much larger datasets.

But it introduces important challenges.

A Broader Perspective

These examples illustrate a broader shift:

Predictions are becoming inputs to public health science.

This creates a need for new statistical tools.

AI as an Epidemiologic Exposure

AI systems themselves can act as exposures.

They influence:

  • access to health information
  • clinical decision-making
  • treatment recommendations

Understanding these impacts requires careful measurement and inference.

Intervening in the Scientific Pipeline

Artificial intelligence is transforming how data are generated and analyzed.

Ensuring that inference remains valid will require new methods, tools, and training.
